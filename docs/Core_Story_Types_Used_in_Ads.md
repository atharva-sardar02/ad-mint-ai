# Core Story Types Used in Ads (Before Storyboarding)

**Purpose:** Reference guide for the pre-story structures directors use to quickly define creative direction before storyboarding begins.

**Audience:** Creative directors, storyboard artists, AI video generation teams, and content creators working on ad campaigns.

**Last Updated:** 2025-01-17

---

## Overview

Before creating detailed storyboards, directors use foundational story structures to establish creative direction. These story types serve as templates that guide visual narrative, emotional tone, and scene progression. Understanding these core types helps teams align on creative vision and streamline the storyboarding process.

This document describes eight fundamental story types commonly used in advertising, each optimized for specific product categories and emotional goals.

---

## 1. The Transformation Story

**Focus:** Something changes — the user, the environment, or the product itself.

**Best For:**
- Beauty products
- Home improvement
- Health and wellness
- Luxury goods

**Visual Pattern:**
- Chaos → Order
- Ordinary → Extraordinary
- Raw ingredients → Refined product
- Before → After

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Extremely visual and easy to storyboard
- Emotionally strong and relatable
- Clear progression creates natural scene breaks
- Demonstrates product value through contrast

**Example Applications:**
- Skincare: Dull skin transforming to radiant glow
- Home renovation: Cluttered space becoming organized sanctuary
- Cooking: Basic ingredients becoming gourmet dish

---

## 2. The Reveal / Discovery Story

**Focus:** The audience gradually learns what the product is through visual buildup.

**Best For:**
- Luxury goods
- Technology products
- Perfume and fragrances
- Automotive

**Visual Pattern:**
- Hint → Build anticipation → Hero product reveal
- Partial views → Full reveal
- Mystery → Clarity

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Allows for visual buildup and cinematic rhythm
- Creates suspense and engagement
- Builds anticipation before product showcase
- Enables dramatic reveal moments

**Example Applications:**
- Luxury watch: Close-ups of craftsmanship → Full timepiece reveal
- New smartphone: Glimpses of features → Complete product unveiling
- Perfume: Abstract imagery → Bottle reveal with signature scent

---

## 3. The Journey or Path Story

**Focus:** A character or object moving through a sequence of environments or emotional states.

**Best For:**
- Fitness and wellness
- Travel and tourism
- Lifestyle brands
- Consumer technology

**Visual Pattern:**
- Starting point → Progression → Destination
- Multiple locations or emotional states
- Movement and transition

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Allows for dynamic scene changes
- Natural scene transitions
- Shows product in various contexts
- Demonstrates versatility and adaptability

**Example Applications:**
- Fitness tracker: Morning run → Workout → Evening recovery
- Travel app: Planning → Journey → Destination
- Lifestyle brand: Day-to-night product usage

---

## 4. The Problem → Solution Story

**Focus:** Show the problem quickly, then demonstrate how the product fixes it.

**Best For:**
- Everyday consumer products
- Mobile applications
- Cleaning supplies
- Kitchen tools

**Visual Pattern:**
- Problem identification → Product introduction → Solution demonstration
- Pain point → Relief → Satisfaction

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Extremely clear and direct
- Sells benefits instantly
- Storyboard is straightforward
- Appeals to practical consumers
- Demonstrates immediate value

**Example Applications:**
- Cleaning product: Stained surface → Product application → Clean result
- App: Frustrating task → App interface → Task completed easily
- Kitchen gadget: Difficult preparation → Tool usage → Effortless result

---

## 5. The Sensory Experience Story

**Focus:** Pure mood, emotion, and texture rather than narrative structure.

**Best For:**
- Perfume and fragrances
- Food and beverage
- Luxury fashion
- Cosmetics

**Visual Pattern:**
- Sensory close-ups → Emotional tone → Product as culmination
- Texture, color, movement
- Atmospheric and abstract

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Relies on visuals and atmosphere, not actors or plot
- Creates emotional connection through sensory appeal
- Allows for artistic and abstract imagery
- Builds brand feeling rather than product features

**Example Applications:**
- Perfume: Flowing fabric, natural elements → Fragrance bottle
- Chocolate: Melting texture, rich colors → Product reveal
- Cosmetics: Skin texture, light play → Product application

---

## 6. The Symbolic Metaphor Story

**Focus:** Abstract imagery representing what the product stands for.

**Best For:**
- Premium brands
- Lifestyle brands
- Eco-friendly products
- Values-driven companies

**Visual Pattern:**
- Symbolic imagery → Product connection
- Abstract concepts made visual
- Metaphorical representation

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Keeps production simple and creative
- Excellent for AI-generated visuals
- Communicates brand values without dialogue
- Allows for artistic interpretation

**Example Metaphors:**
- Flowers = Purity
- Light = Clarity
- Water = Freshness
- Mountains = Strength
- Growth = Progress

**Example Applications:**
- Eco-friendly brand: Nature imagery → Sustainable product
- Premium brand: Luxury symbols → Product reveal
- Wellness brand: Calming imagery → Product integration

---

## 7. The Micro-Drama Story

**Focus:** A tiny narrative — a moment in someone's life.

**Best For:**
- Fashion brands
- Lifestyle products
- Dating apps
- Emotional brands

**Visual Pattern:**
- Character introduction → Moment of action → Resolution
- Human connection and relatability
- Small but meaningful story

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Relatable and human
- Easy to break into 3–5 scenes
- Creates emotional connection
- Shows product in real-life context

**Example Applications:**
- Fashion: Person getting ready for important event
- Cooking product: Chef preparing something beautiful
- Fitness: Runner finishing a race
- Dating app: Two people connecting

---

## 8. The Montage Story

**Focus:** Fast-cut sequence of related shots tied by theme or style.

**Best For:**
- Sports brands
- Youth-targeted products
- Technology
- Energy drinks

**Visual Pattern:**
- Rapid sequence of related shots
- Consistent theme or style
- High energy and dynamic pacing

**Why Directors Use It:**
- Dynamic pacing and visually exciting
- No complex narrative needed
- Captures energy and movement
- Appeals to younger audiences
- Works well for product showcases

**Example Applications:**
- Sports brand: Athletes in various sports → Product logo
- Energy drink: High-energy activities → Product consumption
- Tech product: Various use cases in rapid succession

## Choosing the Right Story Type

When selecting a story type for your ad, consider:

1. **Product Category:** Match story type to industry conventions
2. **Target Audience:** Choose structures that resonate with your demographic
3. **Emotional Goal:** Select types that support your desired emotional response
4. **Production Constraints:** Consider which types work best with available resources
5. **Brand Values:** Align story structure with brand identity

---

## Story Type Selection Matrix

| Story Type | Best For | Emotional Tone | Complexity |
|------------|----------|----------------|------------|
| Transformation | Beauty, Home, Health | Inspiring, Empowering | Medium |
| Reveal/Discovery | Luxury, Tech, Automotive | Anticipatory, Dramatic | Medium-High |
| Journey/Path | Fitness, Travel, Lifestyle | Dynamic, Progressive | Medium |
| Problem → Solution | Consumer Products, Apps | Practical, Satisfying | Low |
| Sensory Experience | Perfume, Food, Fashion | Emotional, Atmospheric | Medium |
| Symbolic Metaphor | Premium, Eco, Values-driven | Abstract, Meaningful | Low-Medium |
| Micro-Drama | Fashion, Lifestyle, Dating | Relatable, Human | Medium |
| Montage | Sports, Youth, Tech | Energetic, Fast-paced | Low |

---

## Integration with Storyboarding

Once a story type is selected:

1. **Break into scenes:** Use the story type's natural structure to define scene breaks
2. **Define transitions:** Each story type suggests transition styles (fade, cut, match)
3. **Establish pacing:** Story types inform rhythm and timing
4. **Guide visual style:** Each type suggests color palettes, camera movements, and composition

## Notes for AI Video Generation

When using AI to generate videos based on these story types:

- **Transformation:** Use consistent character/product across scenes with clear before/after states
- **Reveal:** Build anticipation with partial views before full reveal
- **Journey:** Maintain character/product consistency across location changes
- **Problem → Solution:** Ensure clear visual problem statement before solution
- **Sensory:** Focus on texture, color, and movement details in prompts
- **Metaphor:** Use symbolic imagery that clearly connects to product values
- **Micro-Drama:** Maintain character consistency and clear narrative arc
- **Montage:** Ensure visual consistency in style and theme across rapid cuts
